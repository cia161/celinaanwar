Why Product Management Has Students Hooked—And Why I'm Intrigued Too

November 15, 2024 By Celina Anwar

Product management (PM) has quickly become one of the most appealing career paths for students. It's a role that blends technical expertise, business strategy, and creative problem-solving, offering the chance to shape products. While I'm not completely certain it's the career for me, I can see why it draws so much attention. My experiences as a software engineer intern (SWE), combined with insights from my Technology and Management class and involvement in Product Space, have given me a growing appreciation for the work of a PM.

Why Students Gravitate Toward Product Management

At its core, product management is about making ideas happen. PMs lead teams to develop solutions that align with user needs, business objectives, and technical constraints. This level of ownership is increasingly appealing. Unlike roles with narrowly defined tasks, PMs take charge of a product's lifecycle—from ideation to launch and beyond. For students who enjoy variety and leadership, PM offers a dynamic challenge.

Another key aspect is collaboration. PMs work with cross-functional teams, including engineers, designers, and marketers, to bring products to life. For those who thrive in team environments and enjoy seeing how different perspectives contribute to a final outcome, PM can feel like the perfect fit. Add to this the creativity required to solve user problems and the strategic thinking needed to prioritize features, and it's easy to understand why the role is so attractive.

How I Started Thinking About PM

My interest in PM didn't start immediately. I've primarily worked as a SWE, and I love the problem-solving and technical challenges that come with writing code. Last summer, I worked in software development, and I'll be doing so again this coming summer. I've always enjoyed contributing to the building process, but I started to wonder about the decision-making behind the scenes. Why am I coding this feature? What specific user needs am I fulfilling?

I am taking a class about technology and management and it has helped me connect the dots. Guest speakers who are PMs shared their experiences navigating the competing demands of users, technical teams, and stakeholders. One guest described the balancing act of prioritizing features based on impact and feasibility, and it opened my eyes to the strategic side of product development. It was interesting to hear how PMs help shape the vision for a product while ensuring that it meets real-world constraints.

Learning Through Product Space

Beyond the classroom, my involvement in Product Space, a student club focused on product management, has deepened my understanding of the field. Working on PM-related projects has been a completely different experience from my SWE roles. Instead of focusing on technical implementation, I've spent time brainstorming solutions, conducting user research, and designing strategies to bring ideas to market.

What stands out most to me is how collaborative the process is. Everyone on the team contributes something unique, whether it's technical expertise, design insights, or market analysis. I've loved seeing how these perspectives come together to solve a problem. It's a reminder that PM is as much about people as it is about products—a combination that makes the role feel more dynamic and human compared to writing lines of code.

Where I Stand Now

While I find PM fascinating, I'm not entirely sure it's the path I'll take. I still love the technical challenges of software and the satisfaction of building something from the ground up. My plan is to keep honing my SWE skills while staying open to opportunities to explore PM further.

What draws me to PM isn't just the chance to lead teams or launch products—it's the opportunity to connect my technical background with my interest in business strategy. PM sits at the intersection of everything I enjoy, making it a career path I want to understand better. Even if I don't pursue it right away, I can see myself in a role that bridges technology and strategy down the line.
